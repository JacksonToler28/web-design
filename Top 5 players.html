Matthew Stafford

number 3 Matthew Stafford was the starting quarterback for the Detroit Lions from 2009 to 2020, becoming the face of the franchise during his 12-year tenure. Selected by the Lions as the first overall pick in the 2009 NFL Draft, Stafford quickly established himself as one of the most talented and durable quarterbacks in the league.

On the lions

Throughout his time with Detroit, Stafford displayed impressive arm strength, accuracy, and a knack for leading his team in high-pressure situations. He became known for his ability to throw deep passes and his resilience, often playing through injuries. Stafford holds numerous Lions' franchise records, including passing yards, passing touchdowns, and completions. Despite his individual success, Stafford's time in Detroit was marked by a lack of playoff success. The Lions made the playoffs three times during his career (2011, 2014, and 2016), but they never advanced past the wild-card round. Stafford frequently played for teams that struggled to build a competitive roster around him, which led to a lack of postseason victories.

On the rams

Stafford's leadership and tough play earned him respect across the league, and he became a fan favorite in Detroit. In 2021, after years of enduring frustrations with the team, Stafford was traded to the Los Angeles Rams, where he went on to win his first Super Bowl title in his first season with the Rams. Though his tenure with the Lions did not result in significant playoff success, Matthew Stafford remains one of the most prolific and respected quarterbacks in NFL history, and his legacy in Detroit is that of a fierce competitor and a consistent, high-level performence

Megatron

number 2 is Calvin Johnson, also known as "Megatron," is widely regarded as one of the most dominant wide receivers in NFL history. Born on September 29, 1985, in Newnan, Georgia, Johnson played college football at Georgia Tech before entering the NFL Draft. He was selected by the Detroit Lions as the second overall pick in the 2007 NFL Draft. Standing 6'5" and weighing 237 pounds, Johnson was known for his incredible combination of size, speed, and athleticism. Over the course of his nine-season career (2007–2015), all with the Lions, he established himself as a force to be reckoned with, regularly making spectacular catches and dominating defenders. Johnson set numerous records, including the NFL record for most receiving yards in a single season (1,964 yards in 2012), a mark that still stands today. A six-time Pro Bowl selection and three-time First-Team All-Pro, Johnson consistently ranked among the league's top receivers in both yards and touchdowns. He played in 135 games and recorded over 11,000 receiving yards, 83 touchdowns, and 731 receptions, despite often playing for a team that struggled to compete at a high level.

Retirement

In 2016, at just 30 years old, Johnson surprised many by announcing his retirement from the NFL. His decision was attributed to a combination of physical toll, injuries, and a desire to step away from the game on his own terms. Despite his relatively short career, his legacy as one of the most dominant and gifted wide receivers is undeniable. Johnson was inducted into the Pro Football Hall of Fame in 2021, solidifying his place among the greatest players in NFL history. His blend of size, speed, and hands made him a matchup nightmare and a true legend of the game.

Barry Sanders

number 1 is Barry Sanders is widely regarded as one of the greatest running backs in NFL history. Born on July 16, 1968, in Wichita, Kansas, Sanders played college football at Oklahoma State University, where he won the prestigious Heisman Trophy in 1988. After declaring for the NFL Draft, he was selected by the Detroit Lions in 1989 with the third overall pick. Sanders played his entire 10-season career (1989–1998) with the Lions, where he became known for his elusive running style, exceptional agility, and ability to make defenders miss. Despite playing for a team that often struggled to build a competitive roster around him, Sanders consistently put up remarkable statistics, amassing over 15,000 rushing yards and 99 touchdowns. He was an 10-time Pro Bowl selection and earned First-Team All-Pro honors multiple times. Sanders was also named the NFL's Most Valuable Player (MVP) in 1997. One of his most memorable achievements came in the 1997 season when he rushed for 2,053 yards, becoming only the third player in NFL history to surpass the 2,000-yard mark in a single season.

Retirement

In 1999, at the peak of his career, Sanders stunned the football world by unexpectedly retiring at the age of 31, just 1,457 yards shy of breaking the all-time rushing record held by Walter Payton. His decision to retire early was one of the most shocking in sports history and led to speculation about his reasons, with many believing it was related to his dissatisfaction with the Lions' lack of championship success. Despite his relatively brief career, Sanders is considered one of the best running backs of all time, known for his extraordinary balance, vision, and playmaking ability. He was inducted into the Pro Football Hall of Fame in 2004, cementing his legacy as one of the game's greatest players.
